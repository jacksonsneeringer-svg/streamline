7 Common Freestyle Mistakes
and How to Fix Them

← Technique
Jun 30, 20268 min read

A guy named Pete swam up to me at a masters practice once, completely out of breath after a single 50, and asked why he was so tired when he'd been swimming freestyle his whole life. I watched him do one more length. He was making five of the most common freestyle mistakes at the same time, fighting the water the entire way like it owed him money.

If your freestyle leaves you gassed, slow, or sore in the shoulders, you're probably making a few of these same errors. This is a fix-it guide for adult and masters swimmers who want a freestyle stroke that finally feels smooth instead of like a wrestling match. Here are the seven I see most, and exactly how to clean each one up.

1

Lifting the head to breathe

This is the original sin of freestyle, and almost everyone does it. The instant you lift your head to grab air, your hips and legs sink, your body tilts into a downhill slant, and now you're dragging an anchor. You've turned a sleek shape into a plow.

The fix is to breathe to the side, not the front, and to keep one goggle in the water the whole time. Think about pressing your ear into your extended arm as you rotate. You're looking at the wall beside the pool, not the end of it.

Tip: Imagine a glass of water balanced on the back of your head. Breathe without spilling it. That single image fixes head-lift faster than any cue I know.

Picture it

The mistake: from the side, the head juts up like a driver peering over the hood, a wall of water piling at the forehead, and the hips and feet sagging deep below the surface.

Done right: the waterline cuts across the crown of the head, one goggle stays submerged, and the body lies flat as a plank with the heels just kissing the surface.

2

Crossing over the centerline

Watch a struggling swimmer's hands from above and you'll see them stab into the water past the middle of their body, crossing an invisible centerline that runs from their head to their feet. The result is a fishtail wiggle, your hips snaking side to side, scrubbing off speed with every stroke.

Your hand should enter the water roughly in line with the same-side shoulder, not in front of your nose. Picture two railroad tracks running forward from your shoulders. Each hand stays on its own rail.

If you can't feel it, swim a few lengths of catch-up drill, where one hand waits out front until the other arrives to "tap" it before pulling. It forces a clean, wide entry and kills the crossover almost immediately.

Picture it

The mistake: from directly overhead, each hand spears in past the nose toward the opposite shoulder and the body bends into a snaking S, the hips swishing out behind it.

Done right: two parallel wakes, each hand entering on its own track in line with the shoulder, the spine staying straight as an arrow from head to toes.

3

Dropping the elbow on the catch

Here's where real freestyle speed lives, and where most adult swimmers leave it on the table. After your hand enters, you want a high elbow catch: fingertips angled down, elbow held high near the surface, forearm turned into a big flat paddle that grabs water and pushes it straight back toward your feet.

The common freestyle mistake is dropping the elbow and pulling with a straight arm, like you're sweeping crumbs off a table. A straight arm pushes water down and out instead of back, which lifts you up and moves you nowhere.

To feel the high elbow, try sculling drills or swim with a tennis ball-sized focus on "reaching over a barrel" at the front of each stroke. Adam Peaty built a career on grabbing absurd amounts of water early. You're chasing a smaller version of that same catch.

Picture it

The mistake: an underwater shot of a straight arm windmilling down, palm shoving toward the pool floor, the whole limb slipping through the water like an oar with no blade.

Done right: the elbow rides high near the surface while the forearm and hand hang straight down like a vertical paddle, fingertips pointing at the bottom, hugging an imaginary barrel out front.

4

A lazy, bent-knee kick

If your kick comes from your knees, bicycling along like you're pedaling underwater, you're making drag, not propulsion. A bent-knee kick punches a hole in the water in front of your shins and parachutes you to a stop.

The freestyle kick starts at the hip, runs through a loose knee, and finishes with a relaxed, floppy ankle. Small, fast, mostly from the hip. Your heels should just break the surface and boil the water, not splash like you're stomping grapes.

  • Point the toes. Stiff, flexed feet act like brakes. Tight ankles are the number one reason adult swimmers have a "dead" kick.
  • Kick from the hip. Initiate the motion up high, let the knee bend only slightly as a consequence.
  • Stay narrow. A kick wider than your body line is just more surface area to drag.

Ankle flexibility takes months to improve, so be patient. Years of running and cycling leave most adults with ankles like rusted hinges.

Picture it

The mistake: from the side, the knees bend deep and the feet churn up behind like pedaling a bike, throwing a big white boil of bubbles and going nowhere.

Done right: long legs driven from the hips with only a soft knee bend, loose pointed toes, the heels barely dimpling the surface in a narrow, quiet flutter.

5

Gliding too long (dead spots in the stroke)

Somewhere a well-meaning coach told a generation of swimmers to "glide more," and freestyle has suffered ever since. When you stretch out front and pause, waiting on that lead arm, you create a dead spot where the water grabs you and slows you down. Every stroke becomes a series of little stops and restarts, and restarting a body in water is brutally expensive.

You want continuous, connected propulsion. As one hand finishes the pull at your hip, the other should already be catching up front. Think of a freestyle pull like pedaling a bike: there's never a moment with zero force on the pedals.

This doesn't mean thrashing. Katie Ledecky looks unhurried because she's relentlessly connected, never because she's gliding. Long and smooth, yes. Stalled and waiting, no.

Picture it

The mistake: a freeze-frame with both arms stretched out front at once, the swimmer stalled and parachuting, momentum visibly bleeding away before the next pull begins.

Done right: one hand catching out front the instant the other finishes at the hip, the arms always overlapping in the front quadrant so the body never stops moving.

6

Holding the breath underwater

Plenty of swimmers hold their breath the whole time their face is down, then try to blast out and suck in air during one frantic window. That's a recipe for a breathless, panicked feeling since you end up oxygen-starved and tense.

Exhale steadily through your nose and mouth the entire time your face is in the water. By the time you rotate to breathe, your lungs are nearly empty and ready to fill. The rhythm should feel like a slow, constant hum of bubbles, not a held breath followed by an explosion.

Tip: Practice it standing in the shallow end. Face in, hum out a long stream of bubbles, turn, inhale, repeat. Boring, but it rewires the panic response that wrecks so many freestyle breaths.

Picture it

The mistake: face down with the cheeks puffed and not a single bubble escaping, then a tense, gasping scramble for air the moment the head turns.

Done right: a steady ribbon of bubbles trailing from the nose and mouth the whole time the face is down, so the turn to breathe is a calm, unhurried inhale.

7

No body rotation (swimming flat)

The last common freestyle mistake ties most of the others together. Swimming flat on your stomach, shoulders square to the bottom of the pool, robs you of power and makes breathing a fight. Freestyle is swum on your sides, rotating from one to the other along the line of your spine.

Good rotation, around 30 to 45 degrees to each side, lets your big back and core muscles drive the pull instead of just your arm and shoulder. It also brings your mouth to the air naturally, so you don't have to crank your neck to breathe. Think of your body as a log rolling on a spit, not a raft lying flat.

Drive the rotation from your hips and core, not by throwing your head. The head stays quiet and steady while the body turns underneath it.

Picture it

The mistake: from overhead, the chest and both shoulders lie flat and square to the bottom the whole way, the swimmer plowing along on the belly like a raft.

Done right: the body rolls 30 to 45 degrees onto each side along the spine, one shoulder lifting clear as the other drops, head quiet and steady, like a log turning on a spit.

Pick one and drown it

Don't try to fix all seven at once. You'll overload, swim worse, and quit halfway through a Tuesday practice. Choose the single biggest leak in your stroke, usually the head-lift or the dropped elbow, and obsess over it for two weeks until it's automatic. Then move to the next.

Fix the leaks and the water stops fighting back. That's the whole game.

Pull Buoy 101: How to Actually
Use That Foam Peanut

← Guides
Jul 1, 20267 min read

There's a foam peanut floating in a milk crate at the end of almost every lap pool, and most swimmers either ignore it or use it completely wrong. That foam peanut is a pull buoy, and used well, it's one of the best tools in the building for fixing your freestyle. Used badly, it's a crutch that papers over a sinking-leg problem you should actually be solving. This guide covers what a pull buoy does, how to use one correctly in swim training, the drills worth your time, and the mistakes that quietly hold swimmers back. Whether you're a triathlete, a masters swimmer, or an adult still sorting out your stroke, you'll get more out of that foam peanut by the end of this.

What Is a Pull Buoy and What Does It Actually Do?

A pull buoy is a figure-eight shaped piece of foam you squeeze between your thighs or knees. It floats your hips and legs up toward the surface, which does two things at once.

First, it takes your legs out of the equation. With your lower half floating, you stop kicking and force your arms, shoulders, and core to do all the propulsion. That's the "pull" in pull buoy. It isolates the upper-body engine of your stroke.

Second, it simulates a perfect body position. When your hips ride high and flat, you feel what good freestyle is supposed to feel like, the long, downhill-sliding sensation that strong swimmers get for free and everyone else chases for years.

Tip: The pull buoy is not a flotation device for tired swimmers. It's a training tool with a specific job. If you only reach for it when you want an easy lap, you're wasting it.

Why Swimmers Use a Pull Buoy

The pull buoy earns its spot in the crate for a handful of real reasons, not just because it's there.

  • Builds upper-body strength and feel for the water. Take the kick away and your arms have to pull their own weight, literally. It's resistance training for your catch.
  • Isolates and improves your catch and pull. With legs parked, you can put all your attention on a high elbow catch and a clean finish at the hip, instead of splitting focus with your kick.
  • Teaches better body position. Floating hips show your body what level swimming feels like, and that muscle memory carries over when you take the buoy off.
  • Gives a kick-heavy swimmer a break. Triathletes and adult-onset swimmers often have dead, sinking legs. A pull buoy lets them train stroke volume without burning out a weak kick.
  • Lets you swim through minor leg or ankle issues. Tweaked an ankle? You can still get a quality upper-body workout without kicking.

How to Use a Pull Buoy Correctly

Putting it between your legs is the easy part. Using it well takes a little intention.

1

Place it high, between your upper thighs. The higher up you wedge it, the more it supports your hips and the more it challenges your core to keep everything aligned. Beginners can start lower, between the knees, for more float and stability, then move it up as they get stronger.

2

Squeeze, but stay relaxed. Hold it firmly enough that it doesn't pop out on every stroke, but don't clamp like your life depends on it. A death grip tenses your whole lower body.

3

Keep your legs still and together. No kicking. If your legs are scissoring or kicking anyway, you're cheating the drill. Let them trail like a rudder.

4

Focus on the catch. This is your chance to obsess over a high elbow catch: fingertips down, elbow up near the surface, forearm pressing water straight back to your hip. With no kick to think about, give it your full attention.

5

Hold your body line. Long spine, head neutral, hips high. Use the buoy's float to feel what that alignment should feel like, then try to memorize it.

A pull buoy is the perfect place to drill that high elbow catch, but the catch is only one piece of the puzzle. If your freestyle still fights back the moment the buoy comes off, start by fixing the seven errors that gas out most adult and masters swimmers.

The Best Pull Buoy Drills and Sets

Once you're comfortable, the pull buoy opens up some genuinely useful work.

Straight pull sets. The classic. Swim a set like 6 x 100 with the buoy, focusing on a long, powerful pull and steady stroke count. Try to hold or lower your strokes per length as you go.

Pull with paddles. Add hand paddles to the buoy and you've built a strength session. Paddles increase the surface area your arms move, so your catch and lats work harder. Go easy here at first. Paddles plus buoy is a fast way to aggravate a cranky shoulder if you pile on too much volume too soon.

Band-and-buoy work. Advanced swimmers loop a small ankle band around the feet and use a buoy to fully shut the legs down, exposing every flaw in body position and core tension. This is humbling. The first time most people try it, their hips drop and they feel like they're swimming uphill.

Breathing-pattern pulls. Use a pull set to practice bilateral breathing, every third stroke, since you're not distracted by your kick. It's the perfect time to smooth out a lopsided breathing habit.

Tip: Count your strokes per length during pull sets and write the number down. The pull buoy is a fantastic way to measure whether your catch is getting more efficient over a season.

Common Pull Buoy Mistakes to Avoid

The foam peanut is easy to misuse, and these are the errors I see most at masters practice.

  • Using it as a nap. Pulling should feel like work. If your buoy sets are where you mentally check out, you're missing the point entirely. This is the swimming equivalent of putting an exercise bike in the corner and using it as a coat rack.
  • Kicking anyway. A sneaky little flutter kick defeats the whole purpose. Keep the legs quiet.
  • Living on it. Some swimmers, especially triathletes, get so hooked on the easy high-hip feeling that they avoid swimming without it. The buoy hides your weak kick instead of fixing it. Use it as a tool, not a permanent fix, and keep training your kick separately.
  • Clamping too hard. A tense lower body radiates tension up into your stroke. Relax the squeeze.
  • Ignoring the carryover. The whole point is to take what the buoy teaches and bring it into your full-stroke swimming. If you never connect the two, you're just doing arm laps.

Make the Foam Peanut Work for You

A pull buoy is cheap, nearly indestructible, and most pools hand them out for free, what really matters is how you use it.

Use it to feel high hips and a strong catch, then chase that same feeling when you take it away. Grab the foam peanut from the crate tomorrow, wedge it high, shut your legs off, and spend a few hundred yards thinking about nothing but your catch. Then pull it out and see if your normal freestyle suddenly rides a little higher. That carryover is the entire reason the thing exists.

The first time you put on a pair of swim fins, the pool feels broken in the best way. You push off the wall and suddenly you're flying, your kick finally goes somewhere, and you wonder why you ever swim without them. That feeling is exactly the trap. Swim fins are one of the most useful training tools in the building for fixing your kick, building ankle flexibility, and teaching speed your body has never felt. However, they're also the easiest piece of gear to get hooked on. This guide covers how to use swim fins correctly, the drills that make you faster, and how to avoid leaning on them so hard you go backward.

What Swim Fins Actually Do

Fins are blades that strap to your feet and dramatically increase the surface area pushing against the water. That extra surface does a few things at once, and they all feed each other.

They make you faster, obviously, which lets you practice swimming and kicking at speeds your body otherwise can't reach. That higher velocity teaches your nervous system what a fast tempo and a tight body line feel like. Fins also force your ankles to flex and point under load, which over time builds the ankle flexibility that most adult swimmers desperately lack. And they take pressure off a weak kick so you can hold good body position while you work on your stroke.

Tip: Fins are a teaching tool, not a turbo button. The point is to carry the feeling they create back to your bare-footed swimming, not to swim fast only while you're wearing them.

How to Use Swim Fins Correctly

The blade rewards a good kick and quietly fixes a bad one, if you use it right.

1

Kick from the hip, not the knee. Even with fins on, the motion starts at the hip and runs through a loose knee to a relaxed ankle. Bicycling from the knee with fins just makes a bigger mess.

2

Keep the kick compact and fast. Short blades are built for tempo. Don't let the fins drag you into a slow, lazy, exaggerated kick.

3

Point your toes and let the ankle flex. This is half the reason to wear them. Let the water bend your ankle into a pointed position on the down-kick.

4

Hold a tight body line. Use the extra speed to feel a long, balanced, streamlined position. Press your chest, keep your head neutral, ride high.

5

Use them for focused sets, not the whole practice. Fins are a seasoning, not the main course. A few hundred to a thousand yards in a workout is plenty for most swimmers.

The Best Swim Fin Drills and Sets

Once you're comfortable, fins make hard technique work possible that you couldn't do otherwise.

Underwater dolphin kick. Fins are the best way to build the underwater dolphin kick, the "fifth stroke" that wins races off every wall. Push off in a tight streamline, face down, and dolphin kick from the chest with a loose core. Fins let you feel the rhythm before your bare kick is strong enough to hold it.

Kick on your side. Kick in streamline on one side, bottom arm extended, top arm at your hip, face down except to breathe. It builds a balanced kick in the rotated position you actually swim in, and fins keep you moving fast enough to stay on top of the water.

Fast freestyle with fins. Swim freestyle at a quicker tempo than you can manage on your own. This trains a high stroke rate and lets your arms feel what race speed demands. Keep the stroke long and clean, not frantic.

Vertical kicking. In deep water, kick to keep your head up with arms crossed or overhead, no bottom under your feet. Brutal for the legs and core, and fins let you sustain it long enough to get the benefit.

Tip: Use fins to learn the dolphin kick first, then deliberately practice it without them. The carryover is the entire point, and it only happens if you eventually take the fins off.

Don't Become Fin-Dependent

Here's the failure mode every coach watches for. Fins feel so good that swimmers start reaching for them anytime a set looks hard, and pretty soon they can't kick at all without them. The blade hides a weak kick instead of building one.

  • Cap your fin yardage. Keep fins to a portion of your practice, not the default. If most of your kicking happens in fins, your bare kick will wither.
  • Always follow fin work with bare work. Do a fin drill, then immediately try the same thing without them to transfer the feeling.
  • Don't use them to fake your intervals. Making a tough send-off only because you've got fins on isn't fitness, it's costume. Earn the interval bare-footed when it counts.
  • Watch for lazy knees. If fins turn your kick into a big floppy knee-bend, strip them off and reset your mechanics.

Even the best underwater swimmers in history, the Caeleb Dressels and the Summer McIntoshes who seem to fly off every wall, built those kicks with thousands of yards of un-finned dolphin work too. The fins accelerate the learning. They don't replace it.

Fly, Then Land

Swim fins are cheap relative to how much they teach and they're worth owning. Used well, they build ankle flexibility, teach the dolphin kick, and let you feel a speed that reshapes your whole stroke.

Just remember what that first magic push-off is really for. It's a preview, not a destination. Wear the fins to learn the feeling, then take them off and chase it on your own. The day your bare kick finally remembers what the fins taught it is the day they actually paid off.

This guide covers what a swim snorkel does, how to use one in training, the drills that clean up your freestyle, and the mistakes that make swimmers quit on it before it works.

What a Swim Snorkel Actually Does

A swim training snorkel, the kind that mounts in the center of your forehead rather than off to the side like a scuba snorkel, lets you keep your face down and breathe continuously while you swim. That one change has bigger effects than it sounds.

Breathing is the most disruptive moment in freestyle. Every time you rotate to grab air, you risk lifting your head, crossing over, dropping your hips, or breaking your body line. Take breathing out of the equation and all of those errors lose their excuse. You can hold a perfect, quiet head position lap after lap and put your full attention on your catch, your rotation, and your alignment.

Tip: A snorkel doesn't make you fitter on its own. It removes a distraction so you can fix technique. Think of it as a way to practice good habits without the breathing interruption breaking your concentration.

Why Swimmers Use a Swim Snorkel

The snorkel earns its place for several concrete reasons.

  • Locks in a steady head and body position. With no need to turn for air, your head stays neutral and still, which keeps your hips up and your spine long. Many swimmers feel proper body line for the first time with a snorkel on.
  • Lets you focus entirely on your catch. No breathing to manage means you can obsess over a high elbow catch and a clean pull without splitting your attention.
  • Smooths out a lopsided stroke. Lots of swimmers have a "good side" and a "bad side" created by always breathing the same direction. The snorkel exposes and helps even out that asymmetry.
  • Builds CO2 tolerance and breath control. Restrictor caps that limit airflow turn a snorkel into a respiratory training tool, teaching your body to stay calm with less air.

How to Use a Swim Snorkel Correctly

Strapping it on takes a second. Getting comfortable takes a few laps of patience.

1

Fit the bracket on your forehead and center the tube. The mouthpiece should sit comfortably between your lips and teeth without you clenching. Adjust the head strap snug enough that it doesn't slide.

2

Breathe slow and steady. New snorkel users tend to hyperventilate or panic slightly at first. Take calm, controlled breaths. It feels strange for about three lengths, then it clicks.

3

Keep your head neutral and still. This is the whole benefit. Eyes down at the bottom, neck relaxed, head perfectly quiet. Let your body rotate underneath a steady head.

4

Clear it with a sharp exhale. A little water will sneak in, especially on flip turns. A quick, forceful breath out blasts it through the purge valve. Don't panic, just puff it clear.

5

Skip the flip turns at first. Until you're comfortable, do open turns or just stop at the wall. Flip turns flood the tube and overwhelm beginners. Add them back once breathing feels automatic.

The Best Swim Snorkel Drills and Sets

Once breathing through it feels natural, the snorkel makes precise technique work possible.

Catch-focused freestyle. Swim relaxed freestyle and put 100 percent of your focus on a clean, early, high elbow catch. With no breathing to interrupt you, you can feel every pull from entry to finish.

Body-position and alignment swimming. Hold a long, flat, quiet body line for an entire set and notice how stable you feel without the breathing wobble. This is where swimmers internalize what good position actually feels like.

Single-arm and drill work. The snorkel is perfect for drills that are awkward to breathe through, like single-arm freestyle or fingertip-drag drill. It lets you stay in the drill without breaking rhythm to grab air.

CO2 tolerance sets with a restrictor cap. Advanced swimmers add a cap that limits airflow and swim controlled sets to build comfort with air hunger. Go in gradually and never push this to the point of dizziness.

Tip: Pair the snorkel with a pull buoy and paddles for a pure upper-body technique session. Legs parked, breathing handled, all your focus on building a strong, clean catch.

Common Swim Snorkel Mistakes

The snorkel is easy to give up on, usually for avoidable reasons.

  • Quitting in the first five minutes. It feels claustrophobic and weird at first for almost everyone. Push through three or four lengths before you judge it. The discomfort passes.
  • Lifting the head out of habit. Some swimmers still try to lift to breathe even with a snorkel on, which defeats the entire purpose. Trust the tube and keep your head down.
  • Clenching the mouthpiece. A death grip with your jaw leaves you sore and tense. Hold it lightly.
  • Living in it. Like every tool in this series, the snorkel is for building habits to carry into normal swimming, not a permanent way to avoid breathing practice. You still have to train your actual breathing for racing.
  • Forgetting to rinse it. Pool chemicals and spit build up fast. Rinse it after every use or it gets genuinely gross, the same way a reusable water bottle turns into a science experiment if you ignore it.

Trust the Tube

A center-mount swim snorkel costs about as much as a couple of lunches and teaches more about body position than almost anything else in the pool. The trick is getting past those first claustrophobic laps to the moment it clicks and your stroke goes quiet.

Strap it on, keep your head dead still, and feel what your freestyle does when breathing stops sabotaging it. Then carry that stillness back to your normal swimming, one breath at a time.

What to Look For in Training Fins

Before the list, the rules. A training fin is not a dive fin.

  • Short blades. They keep your kick tempo fast and swimming-specific. Long scuba fins force a slow, exaggerated kick you'll never use in a race.
  • A snug, closed-heel fit. Loose fins blister your heels and slip off on push-offs. You want them to feel like part of your foot.
  • Comfortable material that won't shred your feet over a long set. Neoprene socks help if you're chafe-prone.

Tip: Buy fins that make your kick feel fast, not fins that feel powerful. Power comes from the long-blade dive fins you should be avoiding. Tempo is the goal.

The first time someone hands you a pair of swim paddles, it feels like cheating. Suddenly you're grabbing huge slabs of water and rocketing down the lane like you swallowed an outboard motor. Hand paddles are one of the most powerful tools in swim training and also one of the easiest ways to hurt yourself. What Swim Paddles Actually Do

Hand paddles are flat plastic plates, usually held on with rubber tubing across your fingers and wrist, that increase the surface area of your hand. More surface area means each pull moves more water, which has two effects.

Number one, your stroke gets stronger because your muscles are working against extra resistance, the same logic as adding plates to a bench press. Number two, your stroke gets more honest, because a paddle exaggerates every flaw in your catch. Pull with a dropped elbow or a sloppy hand entry and the paddle will slip, flutter, or yank your wrist sideways, and tell you immediately.

Tip: Paddles are a feedback device first and a strength tool second. If a paddle is sliding around or twisting on your hand, your catch needs fixing before your strength does.

How to Use Hand Paddles Correctly

The paddle rewards good technique and punishes bad technique. Use that.

1

Enter clean, fingertips first. Slide the hand into the water out in front of your shoulder, not crossing your centerline. With a paddle on, a crossover entry torques your shoulder hard.

2

Set an early, high elbow catch. Get your fingertips angled down and your elbow up near the surface fast, then press the whole forearm-and-paddle unit straight back toward your hip. Keep steady pressure on your fingertips the entire pull.

3

Don't muscle it. Let the paddle teach feel, not just build force. Especially at first, swim at a controlled effort and pay attention to where water is slipping off the plate.

4

Keep your stroke long. Paddles tempt people into short, frantic strokes. Resist. Finish each pull all the way past your hip.

5

Build volume slowly. Add paddle yardage gradually over weeks, not all at once.

Protect Your Shoulders

Swimmer's shoulder, an overuse irritation of the rotator cuff and surrounding tendons, is the most common injury in the sport, and paddles are one of the usual suspects. The plate adds load to a joint that's already doing thousands of reps a week.

  • Ramp up slowly. Don't go from zero to a paddle-heavy main set. Add a few hundred yards at a time across several weeks.
  • Stop at the first twinge. Shoulder pain is not a "push through it" situation. Pull the paddles off the moment something feels sharp or pinchy.
  • Fix technique before adding size. A bigger paddle on a bad catch is an injury waiting to happen. Earn the size with clean mechanics.
  • Skip paddles when fatigued or sore. Tired swimmers have sloppy catches, and sloppy catches under paddle load are where shoulders get hurt.

Even Michael Phelps's coach Bob Bowman was famously cautious about loading young swimmers' shoulders with too much paddle and band work too early. If the most decorated swimmer in history took the long road on resistance, you can too.

Earn the Paddle

Hand paddles are a privilege your technique has to earn, not a shortcut around it. Used right, they build a powerful, efficient catch and a stroke that holds together when you're exhausted. Used wrong, they hand you a sore shoulder and a bad month.

Start small, swim clean, ramp up slow, and treat any shoulder pain as a hard stop. Do that, and the next time you peel the paddles off, your bare-handed freestyle will feel like you kept the motor and lost the brakes. That feeling is the whole reason to bother.

What a Kickboard Actually Does

A kickboard is a buoyant foam board you hold out in front of you so your arms are parked and your legs do all the work. It isolates your kick, full stop. With your upper body resting on the board, every bit of propulsion has to come from your hips, legs, and feet.

That isolation is the whole value. Kicking is the part of swimming most adults are worst at, because years of running and cycling leave people with stiff ankles and a habit of kicking from the knee. The board lets you give your kick undivided attention, build leg endurance, and feel where your propulsion is actually coming from.

Tip: A kickboard trains your kick's strength and stamina. It does not, on its own, train good body position. That distinction is why some coaches limit it, and we'll get to that.

Why Swimmers Use a Kickboard

The board earns its place for a few specific reasons.

  • Builds leg strength and endurance. Kicking is exhausting and your legs hold some of your biggest muscles. Dedicated kick sets build the engine that powers your back-half speed.
  • Isolates and improves kick mechanics. With your arms out of the picture, you can focus entirely on kicking from the hip with loose ankles instead of bicycling from the knee.
  • Lets you breathe freely while drilling. Head up on a board, you can watch the pace clock, talk through a set, and grind kick yardage without managing your breath.
  • Gives your shoulders a rest. On a high-volume week, kick sets let you keep training hard while your arms and shoulders recover from pulling.

How to Use a Kickboard Correctly

Holding it is simple. Using it well is where swimmers slip.

1

Hold the board out front, hands near the top. Arms extended, hands gripping the far end or resting on top. Don't bunch up with the board jammed against your chest.

2

Keep your head down between sets of breaths. Many swimmers wreck their position by holding the head straight up the whole time, which buckles the lower back and sinks the hips. Press your chest down and put your face in the water, lifting only to breathe.

3

Kick from the hip, not the knee. The motion starts up high at the hip, travels through a slightly bent, loose knee, and finishes with a floppy ankle. Small, fast, and steady.

4

Point your toes. Flexed, stiff feet act like brakes. Relaxed, pointed ankles let your feet act like fins. Tight ankles are the single most common reason adult swimmers have a "dead" kick.

5

Keep the kick compact. Heels should just break the surface and boil the water, not splash like you're stomping. A kick wider than your body is just drag.

The Best Kickboard Sets

Once your mechanics are decent, the board opens up genuinely hard, useful work.

Straight kick endurance. A set like 8 x 50 kick on a moderate interval, holding a steady, strong rhythm, builds the leg stamina that holds your stroke together late in a race.

Descending kick. Kick a set of 100s and get faster on each one. This teaches you to find another gear with your legs when they're already tired, which is exactly what a race demands.

Sprint kick. Short, all-out efforts like 8 x 25 kick fast with full rest. This builds the explosive leg speed sprinters live on. It also hurts in a very specific, lung-burning way that lets you know it's working.

Streamline kick (no board). The best kicking work often ditches the board entirely, which brings up the honest part of this guide.

Tip: Kick with fins occasionally to feel a faster, more fluid kick and to build ankle flexibility. Just don't let fins become a permanent crutch that hides a weak natural kick.

When to Skip the Kickboard

Here's the part the board's biggest fans don't love to hear. A traditional kickboard, held with your head up and chest raised, trains your kick in a body position you never actually swim in. Real swimming happens with your face down and your body flat and rotating. Kicking upright on a board can even groove a hips-low position, the opposite of what you want.

That's why a lot of modern coaches have shifted toward streamline kicking without a board: arms extended overhead in a tight streamline, face down, kicking on your front or your side, lifting only to breathe. It builds the same leg strength in the exact position you race in. It's harder, less social, and far more useful.

The kickboard still has a place. It's great for beginners learning to kick, for breaststroke and some drill work, for chatty recovery sets, and for grinding pure leg endurance when you want to watch the clock. Just don't let it become the only way you kick. Think of the board like training wheels in the Tour de France sense, useful while you're learning, but not where you want to live.

Use the Board, Don't Lean on It

What matters is intent. Kick hard, kick from the hip with loose ankles, and keep your sets honest instead of using the board as a floating coffee break.

Then, a few times a week, drop the board and kick in streamline with your face in the water, the way you actually swim. Do both, and your legs stop being the thing that gives out in the back half of a race.

← Nutrition
Jul 1, 20266 min read

It's 5:12am, your alarm just went off, and the last thing your body wants is food. I get it. For years I rolled into morning practice running on nothing but the half-life of yesterday's chicken alfredo, then wondered why I fell apart on the main set. What you eat before a morning swim practice decides whether you crush the workout or spend the last 800 fantasizing about the parking lot. This is a practical guide for age-groupers, masters swimmers, and anyone dragging themselves to a too-damn-early practice who wants to know what to eat, how much, and how early to make it actually work.

Why Eating Before a Morning Swim Matters

You wake up with a nearly empty fuel tank. Overnight, your liver burns through most of its stored glycogen just keeping your brain and organs running. By the time you hit the pool, your blood sugar is low and your muscle glycogen is only partly topped off.

Swimming is brutal on that fuel. A hard practice burns somewhere around 500 to 700 calories an hour, mostly from carbohydrates, and your body can't pull oxygen and stored fat fast enough to cover an all-out 100 on the interval. Show up empty and you'll feel it as that heavy-armed, gray-vision wall about 40 minutes in.

The goal before a morning swim is simple: get some fast, easy carbohydrates into your system to lift your blood sugar and prime your muscles, without sitting like a brick in your stomach when you push off the wall.

The Golden Rule: Carbs First, Fat and Fiber Last

Your pre-swim food should be mostly carbohydrates, low in fat and fiber, and modest in protein. Here's why the timing of each matters in the water:

  • Carbs digest fast and go straight to blood sugar and muscle fuel. This is your priority before a morning practice.
  • Fat digests slowly and sits heavy. A bacon-and-cheese situation before a 5:30 practice is a betrayal you'll feel on your first flip turn.
  • Fiber is great for life and terrible right before hard swimming. Big bowls of bran or a giant apple can cramp you or send you off to a bathroom mid-set with your coach eyeing you down the entire time.
  • Protein in a small amount is fine and even helpful, but a heavy protein load digests slowly, so keep it light.

Tip: The closer to practice you're eating, the more it should look like pure sugar and simple carbs. The more lead time you have, the more real food you can get away with.

What to Eat Based on How Much Time You Have

Timing is everything with a pre-swim meal. Match the food to the clock and roll a die for any of these great options.

If you have 60 to 90 minutes (you're an early riser, or it's a meet warm-up):

  • A bowl of oatmeal with banana and a drizzle of honey
  • Toast or a bagel with a thin layer of peanut butter and jam (my personal choice)
  • A bowl of low-fiber cereal with milk
  • Greek yogurt with a banana and a handful of granola

If you have 30 to 45 minutes (the realistic weekday scenario):

  • A banana and a piece of toast with honey
  • A handful of dates or a couple of fig bars
  • A small smoothie with banana, a little milk, and a spoon of honey; toss in a little protein powder if you're feeling fancy
  • A slice of white toast with jam

If you have 10 to 15 minutes (you hit snooze, we've all been there):

  • A banana
  • A few medjool dates
  • An applesauce pouch (the kind marketed to toddlers is unironically perfect)
  • A sports gel or a few swigs of a sports drink

I swam with a 15-year-old named Sophia who swore she "couldn't eat that early" and kept bonking on morning distance sets. She ended up fixing it on accident with two dates and a few sips of my Gatorade in the car. Tiny, fast, no bloat. Her 400 pace jumped within a week. Sometimes the answer isn't more food, it's the right small thing at the right time.

Don't Forget to Drink

You wake up dehydrated after seven or eight hours without water, and you're about to immerse yourself in a pool, where you sweat without ever noticing it. Dehydrated muscles cramp and fatigue early.

Drink 8 to 16 ounces of water or a watered-down sports drink when you wake up. If you're someone whose stomach revolts at solid food before dawn, liquid carbohydrate is your best friend. A smoothie or even diluted juice can deliver fuel and fluid at the same time without the heaviness.

Tip: Keep a banana and a bottle of water on your nightstand. Eat and drink before your feet even hit the floor. By the time you're at the pool, it's already working.

What to Skip Before You Swim

A few foods are landmines before a morning practice, no matter how much you love them.

  • Greasy, high-fat breakfasts. Sausage, bacon, fried eggs, breakfast burritos. They digest like a rock and you'll taste them on your backstroke.
  • Huge amounts of fiber. Save the Special K cereal and the giant raw salad for after.
  • A large coffee on a totally empty stomach. A little caffeine genuinely helps performance and alertness, but black coffee and nothing else always left me jittery and nauseous. If coffee's your ritual, pair it with a banana or toast.
  • Anything brand new on meet morning. Race day is not the time to experiment. The golden rule of competition nutrition is "nothing new," the same lesson every marathoner learns the hard way after one bad gel at mile 18.

Find Your Own Fuel and Lock It In

There's no single perfect pre-swim breakfast, because stomachs are personal. Some swimmers handle oatmeal an hour out like champions. Others can't manage anything heavier than a date and a sip of sports drink. The only way to know yours is to test different options on regular training mornings, never on a meet day, and pay attention to how you feel 45 minutes into the main set.

Once you find the thing that gives you steady energy without weighing you down, treat it like Pavlov's dinner bell and eat it before every morning practice. Your body learns the pattern, your gut stops complaining, and you stop hitting that 40-minute wall. Start with a banana on the nightstand tomorrow. Your 5am self will hate you a little less, and your swimming will thank you on the main set.

Supplements for Swimmers:
What Actually Works (And What's a Waste)

← Nutrition
Jul 5, 20266 min read

The supplement aisle is where swimmers go to spend money on hope. Shiny tubs promising faster recovery, more power, and a leaner body, most of them backed by a photo of someone who has never done a 100 fly in their life. The hard truth about supplements for swimmers is that a handful are genuinely useful, most are marketing, and none of them will fix a training program that's already broken. Here's a breakdown of what actually works, what might help, and what to leave on the shelf, from someone who has watched a lot of athletes waste a lot of money.

Start Here: Food Beats Powder

Before any tub enters the conversation, understand the order of operations. Supplements are the last 5%, and they only matter once the other 95% is handled: consistent training, real food, enough carbohydrates to fuel your yardage, enough protein to repair it, and enough sleep to absorb it all.

A swimmer eating poorly and sleeping six hours will get nothing from a $50 "recovery" powder. A swimmer who has their basics dialed might get a small, real edge from a couple of well-chosen supplements. Get the boring stuff right first, or the rest is just expensive unicorn tears.

Tip: If a supplement promises dramatic results, it's either lying or it's a banned substance. Real, legal supplements offer small margins. Anyone selling you a miracle is selling you a problem.

What Actually Works (Backed by Real Evidence)

A short list of supplements have genuine, well-studied benefits for swimmers.

  • Creatine monohydrate. The most researched sports supplement there is, and it works. It helps with short, explosive efforts, sprint power, and strength gains in the weight room, which matters for sprinters and dryland work. Around 3 to 5 grams a day, the cheap monohydrate form, no fancy blends needed. It may cause a small amount of water-weight gain, which distance swimmers should consider, but for sprinters it's a legitimate edge.
  • Caffeine. A proven performance enhancer for endurance and repeated sprints, and it makes 5am practice survivable. Research supports doses around 3 to 6 mg per kg of body weight before hard efforts. The catch is tolerance and jitters, so don't overdo it, and be careful late in the day so it doesn't wreck the sleep that actually makes you fast.
  • Whey or protein powder. Not magic, just convenient. If you struggle to eat enough protein to repair heavy training, a scoop of whey is an easy, cheap way to hit your target, roughly 1.6 to 2.2 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day from all sources. Real chicken works just as well. The powder is only easier.
  • Vitamin D (if you're deficient). Swimmers train indoors under a roof for months, and many are genuinely low on vitamin D, which affects bone health and muscle function. Worth a blood test, and worth supplementing if you're actually deficient. Don't megadose blindly.

What Might Help (The Maybe Pile)

These have some evidence or specific use cases, but they're not slam dunks.

  • Beta-alanine. May help buffer the muscle burn in efforts lasting one to several minutes, which covers a lot of swimming races. The evidence is decent but modest, and it causes a harmless but weird skin-tingling sensation that freaks people out the first time.
  • Beetroot juice / nitrates. Some evidence for improved endurance efficiency. Real but small, and you have to stomach a lot of beet juice, which is its own commitment.
  • Fish oil / omega-3s. General health benefits and possible anti-inflammatory effects. Reasonable to take for overall health, but don't expect a performance jump.
  • A basic multivitamin. Insurance, not a performance tool. Fine as a safety net if your diet has gaps, useless as a magic bullet.

What's a Waste of Money

Now the stuff to walk past.

  • BCAAs (branched-chain amino acids). If you eat enough total protein, and most swimmers do, BCAAs add almost nothing. You're paying premium prices for amino acids already in your chicken.
  • "Fat burners" and metabolism boosters. Overpriced caffeine with a scary ingredient list and wild claims. Skip them. Swimming already burns 500 to 700 calories an hour, and no pill beats that.
  • Testosterone boosters and most "natural anabolics." They don't work, and some are contaminated with things that will end a competitive career.
  • Exotic recovery blends with proprietary labels. If the label hides the doses behind a "proprietary blend," you don't know what you're getting, which is exactly the point. Real supplements list real amounts.

A Word on Banned Substances and Contamination

For any swimmer who competes under drug-testing rules, this part isn't optional. The supplement industry is loosely regulated, and products get contaminated with banned substances more often than people think. Athletes have failed tests and lost eligibility over a tainted tub they bought in good faith.

If you're subject to testing, only use supplements that are third-party certified, look for NSF Certified for Sport or Informed Sport on the label. Those programs test the actual product for banned substances. "It was in my protein powder" is not a defense that has saved anyone's career.

The Bottom Line

Supplements for swimmers are a small tool, not a shortcut. If you want the realistic short list: creatine if you sprint or lift, caffeine before hard sessions, protein powder if you can't eat enough, and vitamin D if a test says you're low. That's most of what's worth your money, and it's a lot cheaper than the wall of tubs promising the moon.

Everything else is either a maybe or a marketing budget. Spend your money on good food, protect your sleep like it's a supplement (it's better than all of them), and treat the powders as the tiny finishing touch they actually are. The fastest swimmers I ever coached weren't the ones with the best cabinet of pills. They were the ones who ate real meals and went to bed on time, unglamorous as that is.

This is general information, not personalized medical or nutrition advice. Check with a doctor or a registered sports dietitian before starting any supplement, especially if you compete under anti-doping rules.

She did everything right. And in the final week she got scared, swam easy every day to "save herself," and showed up flat. That's a broken taper, and it's the most common way fast swimmers leave time in the pool. This is a guide for competitive and masters swimmers who want a race-week taper that actually delivers a best time, not a meltdown, and it starts with understanding what the taper is doing to your body in the first place.

"A real taper asks you to believe in work you can no longer touch, then sharpen it and get out of the way."

What a race-week taper actually does

A taper is a planned drop in training volume that lets your body cash in the work you already banked. All season you've been carrying chronic fatigue: micro-damaged muscle, drained glycogen, a nervous system running on fumes. None of your fitness vanishes when you back off. The fatigue fades faster than the fitness does, and what surfaces underneath is the swimmer who was fast all along and finally gets to feel it.

The science is unusually tidy for this stuff. Bosquet's 2003 meta-analysis found performance improves about 3 percent during a well-built taper, with the biggest gains coming from cutting volume by roughly 41 to 60 percent while keeping intensity high. Three percent sounds like rounding error until you put it on a 100 freestyle, where it's worth more than a second. That's the wall between making the A final and watching it from the warm-down pool.

The operative word is volume. You cut how much you swim. You guard how fast.

The number one taper mistake: going soft

Most blown tapers die the same way. A swimmer hears "rest" and turns every set into a slow aerobic float, swimming gentle laps all week and arriving at the meet feeling like a deflated pool toy.

Your muscles hold a memory for pace. Spend race week cruising at 70 percent and your body forgets what race effort feels like, your stroke rate drifts, and your turnover goes to sleep. Intensity is what protects your speed. Volume is the part you're allowed to throw away.

Tip: During taper week your total yardage drops hard, but you keep hitting race-pace and faster-than-race efforts in short bursts with long rest. Quality reps with full recovery, never grinding sets.

I coached a masters swimmer named Dana, 46, who had just qualified for nationals in the 50 fly for the first time in her life. The week before, she got nervous and swam easy all week to protect herself. She showed up flat, added four tenths, and was livid in the parking lot afterward. The next season we kept her sprinting straight through taper (sharp 25s at race pace with a wall of rest between them) and she dropped six tenths and made the podium. Same body, same training block. The only change was that we kept the engine revving instead of letting it idle.

How long should your race-week taper be?

"Race week" implies seven days, and for a single big meet that's about right for most age-groupers and masters swimmers. Elite swimmers carrying enormous training loads often taper two to three weeks because they have far more fatigue to shed. The bigger your normal volume, the longer the unload.

For a one-week taper aimed at sprint and mid-distance events, here's a framework that holds up:

1

Seven to five days out

Cut to roughly 60 percent of normal volume. Keep two real race-pace sets with long rest. This is where you remind the body what fast feels like.

2

Four to three days out

Drop to around 40 to 50 percent. Short race-pace efforts, sharp starts and turns, a few all-out 25s. Done lifting anything heavy by now.

3

Two days out

Light swim of maybe 1,500 to 2,500 yards with a handful of race-pace 50s tucked into easy swimming. Dial in stroke rate.

4

Day before

Short and loose. Starts, turns, a couple of 25s to feel snappy, then climb out. Twenty to thirty minutes is plenty.

5

Race day

A full warm-up. Do not skip it because you feel good. You want to reach the blocks already primed.

A 1500 freestyler and a 50 sprinter should not taper identically. Distance swimmers and triathletes can hold a little more volume deeper into the week, because their events reward aerobic durability over raw pop. I once watched a triathlete copy a sprinter's taper to the letter, then fade in the back half of her open-water swim because she'd rested away the engine she actually needed.

The taper crazies are real

Nobody warns you about the head game, so I will. Around day three or four you will feel awful: heavy, sluggish, slow, and dead certain you've lost every ounce of fitness. Your sleep gets strange. Phantom aches show up. You watch a lanemate rip a practice 50 and spiral into quiet doom.

This is normal and it happens to everyone. It happened to me before every major meet of my career, and I never once got used to it. It's the athletic version of the final season of a show you love, where you're so close to the payoff that the waiting becomes the worst part.

The reason is recalibration. As fatigue lifts, your sense of effort gets scrambled before it resettles into real freshness. The flatness in the middle of taper is not lost speed. It's the awkward middle chapter before the speed shows up.

Tip: Do not add a hard "confidence set" mid-taper because you feel slow. That panic set has wrecked more meets than I can count. The bank is full. Stop trying to make one more deposit the day before the withdrawal.

What to do outside the pool during taper week

Taper isn't only a pool program. The habits around it matter more this week than any other, and three of them carry most of the weight.

Sleep like it's your job. The week your training drops is the week your body does its repair work. The nervous-system recovery that makes you fast happens while you're unconscious, so chase more sleep, not less.

Keep carbs steady and top off the tank. You're swimming less, so you don't need a dramatic feast, but you want glycogen full by race day. The night before is not the moment to try the new spicy place you've been eyeing.

Change nothing. No new dryland move, no new pre-race supplement, no tech suit you've never worn. Race week executes the plan. It doesn't audition ideas.

I had a college teammate who decided taper week was the perfect time to deep-clean his apartment and reorganize every closet because he had "all this energy." He stood up at conference with quads full of lactic acid from six hours of squatting over a vacuum. Taper energy is a loan against your race. Spend it in the pool or don't spend it at all.

Trust the build, then trust the rest

Go back to that swimmer behind the blocks with the sandbag legs. Her problem was never the training. It was the last seven days, when fear told her to do less of everything instead of less of one thing. A real taper asks you to believe in work you can no longer touch, then sharpen it and get out of the way.

So when day four lands and you feel like a stranger in your own body, don't panic and don't add yardage. Sharpen, sleep, and let the fatigue drain out from under your speed. Then step up, find your feet on the block, and go collect what you already earned.

Ask a swim team when the "real" practice is and you'll start a small war. The morning crowd swears the dawn session builds character and separates the committed from the tourists. The afternoon crowd points out, reasonably, that human beings are physically stronger later in the day. Both are right, which is why the morning vs. afternoon swim practice debate never really ends. If you can only train once a day, or you're deciding which session to prioritize, here's what the science and the pool deck actually say about training in the morning versus the afternoon.

What Your Body Is Doing at Each Time of Day

Your body runs on a circadian rhythm, and it does not treat 5:30am and 5:30pm the same.

Core body temperature, muscle flexibility, reaction time, and peak power output all tend to hit their high point in the late afternoon and early evening, roughly between 4pm and 7pm. Your body is literally warmer and more pliable, your grip is stronger, and your nervous system fires faster. Studies on athletic performance have found small but real advantages, often a few percent, for maximal efforts done in the late afternoon.

In the morning, your core temperature is at its lowest, your spine is stiff from a night lying down, and your body is still shaking off sleep inertia. You are, physiologically, a colder and slightly weaker version of your evening self for the first hour or so.

Tip: If you race at a specific time of day, train at that time when you can. Your body adapts to perform when it's used to performing. A swimmer who only ever trains at night can feel flat in prelims that start at 9am.

The Case for Morning Practice

Dawn practice has real advantages that have nothing to do with suffering for its own sake.

  • It builds mental toughness that transfers to racing. Getting in cold water at 5:30 when every cell wants to stay in bed is a rehearsal for doing hard things you don't feel like doing. That muscle matters when you're behind at the 150 wall.
  • Meets often start in the morning. Prelims frequently run early. Training your body to swim fast before noon means you're not stunned when the meet demands it.
  • It's done before life interferes. Nothing comes up at 5am. No work emergency, no dinner plans, no fatigue from a brutal day. The session gets banked before the world wakes up.
  • The pool is calmer. Fewer people, quieter deck, and a kind of meditative focus you rarely get in a packed afternoon session.

The catch is fueling. You wake up with a nearly empty tank and a cold body, so a proper warm-up and even a few bites of fast carbohydrate matter more than they do later.

I trained mornings for most of my career, and I'll be honest, I never once loved the alarm. What I loved was the feeling at 7am of already having done the hardest thing I'd do all day. There's a specific smugness to eating breakfast having already swum 6,000 yards. It's not a good look, but it's a real feeling.

The Case for Afternoon Practice

The afternoon session has physiology on its side, and it's a strong argument.

  • You're genuinely stronger and faster. Warmer muscles, higher power output, quicker reactions. Your best times in practice will usually come in the afternoon, and hard speed and power sets land better here.
  • You're properly fueled. You've eaten a few times by afternoon, so your glycogen tank is full and your energy is steady. No 5am nausea to manage.
  • Lower injury risk on hard efforts. Warm, loose muscles handle sprint and power work better than cold morning ones, which reduces the tweaks and strains that come from going hard on a stiff body.
  • Better skill learning. Fine technique work can stick better when you're alert and not fighting sleep inertia.

The downside is life. Afternoons are where fatigue, work stress, obligations, and plain old motivation collapse can sabotage a session. The 5am practice is protected by the fact that nothing else is happening. The 5pm practice has to compete with your entire day.

So Which One Is Better?

For pure physical performance in a single session, afternoon wins. Your body is built to move better later in the day, and if your only goal is the fastest possible practice swims, that's when to do them.

For competitive swimmers preparing to race, the answer is less tidy. Most serious programs train mornings and afternoons precisely because you need both: the toughness and early-swim adaptation of mornings, and the quality and power of afternoons. If you have to choose based on your meet schedule, train when you'll actually race.

For fitness swimmers and adult-onset athletes, the best practice is the one you'll consistently show up for. A physiologically perfect 5pm session you skip three times a week loses to a slightly groggy 6am session you never miss. Consistency beats optimization, the same way the best workout program is the one you'll actually follow, not the one that looks best on paper.

Pick Your Poison and Own It

The morning vs. afternoon swim practice debate has no universal winner, only a right answer for your goals and your life. Racing early meets? Get comfortable swimming fast in the morning. Chasing your best training times? Ride the afternoon peak. Just trying to stay fit and sane? Train whenever you'll reliably keep showing up.

The dawn purists and the afternoon physiologists will keep arguing in the parking lot forever. Let them. Find the session you can commit to, then go make it count, and let the smug 7am breakfast take care of itself.
